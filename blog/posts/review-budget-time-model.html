How Much of Today Did You Spend Replaying Yesterday?

December 15, 2025

Your Life's midpoint is 18

If you're past 18, you've already lived more than half your life—subjectively. The years from 4 to 18 feel as long as the years from 18 to 80. Not because time literally changes, but because of how we experience it.

This is a very interesting idea from the following two videos. They're in Chinese, but the visuals are pretty self-explanatory:

"人生进度条" — why time feels faster as we age

"世界是对数的……吗？" — why log-ish effects show up everywhere

Why does time seem to accelerate? The standard answer is logarithmic time perception: our subjective experience of time follows a logarithmic scale rather than a linear one. A year at age 10 feels much longer than a year at age 50, because on a log scale, the "distance" from 10 to 11 is far greater than from 50 to 51.

Here's a simple consequence: if subjective time is logarithmic, when do you reach the midpoint of your life? Not at 40. To find the subjective midpoint between ages \(a\) and \(b\), we solve: \[ \log(m) - \log(a) = \log(b) - \log(m) \implies m = \sqrt{a \cdot b} \] If conscious memory starts around age 4 and you live to 80, then \(m = \sqrt{4 \times 80} = \sqrt{320} \approx 18\). Half your subjective life is over before you finish high school.

Why does this logarithmic pattern appear? Network science offers a clue. When a new node joins a network, it connects preferentially to well-connected nodes. The probability of connecting to node \(i\) is proportional to its current degree: \[ \Pi(k_i) = \frac{k_i}{\sum_j k_j} \] Larger hubs attract more connections, becoming even larger. This "rich get richer" dynamic produces a power law distribution: \(P(k) \propto k^{-\gamma}\). Unlike normal distributions, power laws have long tails: a few nodes dominate with many connections, while most have few.

Network Science of our Memory

Now apply this to our memory. If we consider each new event as a node, it connects to existing memories: comparing, relating, anchoring. A child with few stored experiences can encode a new event with little overhead. An adult with decades of memories must link each new moment to a vast existing network. More attention flows to integration; less remains for the experience itself. The present shrinks. Time accelerates.

In this blog, we define this integration process as replay.

Running conversations again. Re-living proud moments. Feeling regret. Comparing today to childhood. Rewriting decisions that can't be changed. It leaks into walking, showering, commuting, staring at the ceiling, pretending to listen.

How much of today did you spend replaying yesterday—or five years ago?

I don't have a clean answer. But I want to sketch a model and see what falls out.

  • Can we formulate this phenomenon into a graphical model?
  • From this formulation, can we derive how much time to spend reviewing, and what to review?
  • Can we extend this to decision-making, and how might we test it?

Formulation and modeling

The underlying system here (brain + environment + memory) is highly complex and far from linear, but our observations still arrive sequentially. So it makes sense to start from a sequential decision-making framework, and a POMDP is a powerful choice for writing that down. I'm going to use it mainly to fix notation.

A POMDP is the tuple \[ \mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{O}, T, Z, R, \gamma). \]

  • States \(\mathcal{S}\): latent states \(s_t \in \mathcal{S}\).
  • Actions \(\mathcal{A}\): actions \(a_t \in \mathcal{A}\).
  • Observations \(\mathcal{O}\): observations \(o_t \in \mathcal{O}\).
  • Transition model \(T(s' \mid s,a)\): dynamics \(s_{t+1} \sim T(\cdot \mid s_t, a_t)\).
  • Observation model \(Z(o \mid s,a)\) (often \(Z(o \mid s)\)): emissions \(o_{t} \sim Z(\cdot \mid s_t, a_{t-1})\).
  • Reward \(R(s,a)\): scalar reward \(r_t = R(s_t, a_t)\).
  • Discount \(\gamma \in [0,1)\): time preference over future rewards.

Since the state is hidden, we track a belief \(b_t(s) = \mathbb{P}(s_t=s \mid h_t)\), where the history \(h_t = (o_1,a_1,\dots,o_t)\). After action \(a_t\) and a new observation \(o_{t+1}\), the belief update is: \[ b_{t+1}(s') = \eta \, Z(o_{t+1}\mid s', a_t)\sum_{s\in\mathcal{S}} T(s'\mid s, a_t)\, b_t(s), \] with normalizer \(\eta\).

A (history- or belief-based) policy \(\pi\) selects actions, e.g. \(a_t \sim \pi(\cdot \mid b_t)\). The usual objective is: \[ \max_{\pi}\; \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\Big[\sum_{t=1}^{\infty}\gamma^{t-1} R(s_t, a_t)\Big]. \]
