Survey: Bridging the Simulation-to-Reality Gap

April 1, 2026

1. Introduction and Problem Definition

Training robot controllers in the real world is slow, expensive, and dangerous. A single reinforcement learning (RL) policy for dexterous manipulation may require millions of environment interactions, each carrying the risk of hardware damage, human injury, or simply wasted hours waiting for a robot arm to reset. Simulation offers an escape: physics engines like MuJoCo and NVIDIA PhysX can run thousands of parallel environments on a single GPU, compressing years of experience into hours of wall-clock time. But policies that master simulated tasks routinely fail when transferred to physical hardware. The gap between what a simulator models and what actually happens on a real robot is the sim-to-real gap, and closing it is one of the central challenges of modern robotics.

The term "reality gap" was coined by [15] in the context of evolutionary robotics, where controllers evolved in a simulated Khepera robot failed on the physical platform. Three decades later, the problem persists, though the tools and techniques for addressing it have grown enormously more sophisticated.

What makes it hard

The sim-to-real gap is not a single problem but a constellation of mismatches. Following the taxonomy proposed by [2], these mismatches fall into four categories:

  1. Dynamics mismatch. Simulators approximate physics with rigid-body contact models, simplified friction cones, and idealized actuators. Real robots have tendon compliance, backlash, motor heating, and contact dynamics that no simulator fully captures. This is especially acute for contact-rich manipulation, where small errors in friction or stiffness compound into qualitatively different outcomes.
  2. Perception mismatch. Rendered images differ from camera images in lighting, texture, reflections, lens distortion, and noise characteristics. Depth sensors in simulation produce clean point clouds; real depth cameras suffer from missing returns on reflective or transparent surfaces. Even proprioceptive sensors (joint encoders, IMUs) have noise profiles and latencies that simulators approximate imperfectly.
  3. Actuation mismatch. The commanded torque is not the delivered torque. Real actuators have current limits, thermal throttling, gear friction, and communication delays. Many commercial robots run proprietary low-level controllers whose behavior is opaque to the researcher. [6] found that simulating an ANYmal quadruped required setting the virtual mass to 8,500 kg (versus the real 730 kg) to match real locomotion dynamics, illustrating how far default simulator parameters can be from reality.
  4. System-level mismatch. Real deployments introduce distributed computing latency, safety watchdogs that override commands, sensor synchronization jitter, and environmental factors (temperature, humidity, surface wear) that change over time.

The fundamental tension is this: making the simulator more faithful is expensive and never complete, while making the policy more robust risks sacrificing performance by being conservative. The field has spent three decades exploring different points on this trade-off curve.

These families are not mutually exclusive. The most successful modern systems combine multiple approaches. 3. Timeline and Evolution

The foundations (1995-2016)

The story begins with [15], who showed that adding calibrated noise to a Khepera robot simulator significantly improved transfer of evolved controllers. Their "envelope of noise" hypothesis, that a controller robust to realistic noise in simulation will also be robust to the discrepancies between sim and real, planted the seed for what would become domain randomization two decades later.

[20] reframed the problem as multi-objective optimization, jointly optimizing task fitness and a transferability metric. Their insight that simulation efficiency and real-world transferability are conflicting objectives was prescient, though their evolutionary robotics framing was soon eclipsed by deep RL.

The domain randomization era (2017-2019)

The field underwent a phase transition in 2017 with two concurrent papers. [31] trained a collision-avoidance policy for indoor quadrotor flight entirely on randomized renderings of 3D CAD models, achieving zero-shot transfer to real environments. [35] formalized and named "domain randomization" (DR), the technique of randomizing visual parameters like textures, lighting, camera poses, and object appearances during training so that the real world appears as just another variation of the simulator. Tobin et al.'s paper became the most-cited work in the sim-to-real literature, with over 3,300 citations.

The key counterintuitive insight was that deliberately unrealistic, randomly-textured scenes transfer better than carefully crafted realistic ones. The network cannot overfit to any particular visual style and is forced to learn geometry-invariant features. This was soon extended from visual parameters to physics parameters by [26], who randomized masses, friction, damping, and actuator parameters during training, enabling zero-shot transfer of a robotic pushing policy.

The landmark demonstration of this era was OpenAI's dexterous manipulation work. [3] combined massive-scale domain randomization over 50+ physical and visual parameters with distributed RL to train a Shadow Hand to reorient objects, transferring zero-shot from simulation to a real 24-DoF robot hand. [1] pushed further, solving a Rubik's Cube with the same hand by introducing Automatic Domain Randomization (ADR), which progressively increases environment complexity as the agent improves rather than requiring manual specification of randomization ranges. ADR-trained policies exhibited emergent meta-learning at test time, adapting on-the-fly to real-world physics never seen during training.

The privileged learning revolution (2020-2022)

While domain randomization was scaling up, a parallel paradigm was emerging. [27] introduced the asymmetric actor-critic, where the critic (the value function network used only during training) receives full simulator state while the actor (the policy deployed on the real robot) receives only camera images. This formalized the idea that training and deployment can use different information.

[7] crystallized this into the "learning by cheating" framework for autonomous driving in CARLA (an open-source urban driving simulator built on Unreal Engine 4 [10]). First, a "cheating" agent is trained with access to ground-truth privileged state like a bird's-eye-view map. Then, a sensorimotor student that operates from raw camera input alone is trained to imitate the cheating teacher via DAgger (Dataset Aggregation, an online imitation learning method where the student collects its own data and queries the expert for corrections). Though demonstrated in simulation only, this two-stage pipeline became the dominant approach for sim-to-real robotics.

[22] applied this pipeline to quadrupedal locomotion with striking results. A teacher policy trained with access to ground-truth terrain profiles, contact forces, and friction coefficients was distilled into a proprioceptive student using a Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN, a 1D convolutional architecture that processes temporal sequences). The student was deployed zero-shot on an ANYmal quadruped across mud, snow, rubble, and vegetation, and was even used in the DARPA Subterranean Challenge with zero failures across four 60-minute missions. This paper established that training on rigid procedural terrain in simulation can transfer to deformable, cluttered natural environments, a result that challenged assumptions about how faithful a simulator needs to be.

[21] introduced Rapid Motor Adaptation (RMA), adding an explicit adaptation module. A base policy is trained with privileged access to ground-truth environmental parameters compressed into a low-dimensional latent vector. A separate adaptation module, a lightweight 1D CNN over half a second of proprioceptive history, is trained to predict this latent vector from sensor data alone. At deployment, the adaptation module runs asynchronously from the base policy, enabling real-time adjustment without any environmental knowledge or fine-tuning. RMA achieved near-oracle performance on an A1 quadruped across diverse terrains, adapting in under one second compared to minutes for prior methods.

Maturation and diversification (2023-present)

The current period is characterized by theoretical deepening, tool consolidation, and the emergence of alternative paradigms.

On the theory side, [8] provided the first sharp bounds on the sim-to-real gap under domain randomization, proving that transfer can succeed without any real-world samples under mild conditions. Their analysis revealed that history-dependent policies (those with memory, like LSTMs) are provably necessary for domain randomization to work, giving theoretical grounding to the empirical observation that recurrent policies transfer better.

[6] challenged the dominance of domain randomization by demonstrating that careful system identification of actuator dynamics, requiring only 20 seconds of encoder data per joint, can replace broad randomization entirely. Their PACE framework deployed zero-shot on 13 different legged robot platforms without any dynamics randomization, achieving a 32% reduction in cost of transport on ANYmal. This is a significant result because it suggests that for locomotion at least, the field may have been over-relying on randomization when precise identification is both feasible and superior.

On the tools side, NVIDIA Isaac Lab and Google DeepMind's MuJoCo Playground have emerged as the two dominant GPU-accelerated frameworks for sim-to-real research, each with comprehensive domain randomization pipelines and deployment workflows (discussed in Section 5).

The most recent frontier is the integration of foundation models. [9] survey how large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) are being used to bridge specific components of the gap: VLMs as semantic anchors for the observation gap, LLMs for automated reward design to bridge the reward gap, and LLM-enhanced forward models for the dynamics gap. [40] demonstrated that natural language descriptions can serve as a domain-invariant signal to bridge the visual sim-to-real gap, outperforming established methods like CLIP and R3M by 25-40% on manipulation tasks.

4. Key Papers and Contributions

4.1 Domain Randomization

The foundational papers. [35] and [31] independently demonstrated that visual randomization enables zero-shot sim-to-real transfer. Tobin et al. achieved ~1.5 cm localization error and 95% grasping success on a Fetch robot using only non-photorealistic, randomly-textured MuJoCo renderings. Their ablation showed that texture diversity is critical (performance degrades below ~1,000 unique textures) and that distractor objects are essential for cluttered-scene robustness. Sadeghi and Levine showed the principle extends to safety-critical platforms, training collision-avoidance for quadrotors that navigated around furniture, through windows, and up staircases never seen in training.

[26] extended randomization from visual appearance to physics dynamics, randomizing 95 parameters including link masses, joint damping, friction coefficients, and actuator parameters. The key finding was that an LSTM-based policy naturally learns to infer current dynamics from its observation history, performing implicit online system identification without any explicit estimation module. This implicit identification through memory became a recurring theme in subsequent work.

Automatic domain randomization. Manual DR requires guessing appropriate parameter ranges, which doesn't scale. [1] introduced ADR, which starts from a single calibrated environment and automatically widens randomization ranges whenever the policy's performance exceeds a threshold. For the Rubik's Cube task, ADR achieved 15x better real-world performance than manual DR (26.8 vs. 1.8 mean consecutive successes). The paper showed a clear monotonic relationship between ADR entropy (the breadth of the randomization distribution, measured in nats-per-dimension) and real-world performance, suggesting that broader training distributions are generally better.

[34] proposed DORAEMON, which formulates DR as constrained entropy maximization: maximize the entropy (a measure of spread or disorder) of the dynamics parameter distribution while maintaining policy success probability above a threshold. Unlike ADR, DORAEMON works with arbitrary distribution families (not just uniform bounds), requires no real-world data, and naturally produces a curriculum where the distribution starts narrow and progressively widens as the policy improves. On a 17-dimensional randomization space for box pushing, DORAEMON achieved 60% real-world success versus 0% for fixed DR. The principled formulation is elegant, though the real-world validation remains limited to a single task.

Data-driven distribution fitting. Rather than expanding ranges blindly, [33] proposed DROPO, which fits DR distributions from a small offline dataset of real-world trajectories using a likelihood-based objective. They proved that the L2-based approach used by prior work (DROID) provably collapses distributions to point estimates, losing the variance that makes policies robust. DROPO achieved 2 cm error on a pushing task from a single 13-second demonstration, halving the error of DROID. This represents a middle ground between zero-shot DR and full system identification: use a small amount of real data to calibrate the randomization distribution rather than the simulator itself.

Theoretical grounding. [8] provided the first formal guarantees for domain randomization. Their analysis reveals that the sim-to-real gap under DR scales as \(O(\sqrt{H})\) with the horizon length \(H\) under mild conditions, and this bound is tight. Two results stand out. First, history-dependent policies are provably necessary: without memory, a policy cannot implicitly identify which environment it is in, and the gap can be \(\Omega(H)\) (linear in the horizon, meaning it grows without bound). This validates the widespread empirical practice of using LSTMs or temporal convolutions for sim-to-real. Second, the communicating MDP assumption (that every state is reachable from every other state within a bounded number of steps, quantified by the "diameter" \(D\)) is essential: without it, domain randomization fails completely. This gives practitioners a concrete diagnostic: if your real-world MDP has states that are unreachable from common starting configurations, DR may not help.

4.2 System Identification and Sim Calibration

The alternative to making the policy robust is making the simulator accurate. Classical system identification (SysID), the process of estimating mathematical model parameters from measured input-output data, has a long history in control theory. Modern approaches automate and extend it.

[11] introduced the Search Param Model (SPM), which reframes simulator parameter calibration as binary classification rather than regression. Given observation-action sequences from the real robot, the SPM predicts whether each simulator parameter is higher or lower than its current value, then iteratively adjusts. The insight that binary comparison is fundamentally easier than continuous regression from high-dimensional observations is both simple and powerful. SPM achieved 90% success on a cabinet-sliding task where standard DR scored 0%, using only RGB trajectories with no state instrumentation.

[14] introduced COMPASS, which learns a differentiable causal graph (a directed acyclic graph encoding which simulator parameters causally influence which aspects of the trajectory discrepancy) over simulator parameters and the sim-real gap. The key finding is that most simulator parameters are irrelevant to any given task: causal discovery typically identifies 4-6 out of 20+ parameters that actually drive the gap. Optimizing only these causally relevant parameters improved success rates to ~90% while using 45% fewer rollouts than SimOpt (a prior gradient-based simulator parameter optimization method). The causal structure also provides interpretability, letting engineers see exactly which parameters cause failures.

[6] took system identification to its logical extreme, demonstrating that for legged locomotion, domain randomization is unnecessary if the actuator model is sufficiently accurate. Their PACE framework fits a compact per-joint parameter set (armature inertia, viscous damping, Coulomb friction, position bias, plus one global communication delay) from ~20 seconds of suspended-robot chirp signal data. The first-principles PMSM (Permanent Magnet Synchronous Motor) energy model simultaneously closes the sim-to-real gap and provides a physically meaningful energy reward signal. The results are striking: 32% cost-of-transport reduction on ANYmal, 4,120m range per charge (versus 2,640m prior), and zero-shot deployment on 13 diverse platforms from quadrupeds to humanoids to dexterous hands. This paper is perhaps the strongest argument that for well-characterized actuation systems, precise identification beats broad randomization.

4.3 Domain Adaptation

Domain adaptation methods learn a mapping between simulated and real observations, typically targeting the visual gap.

[16] introduced RCAN (Randomized-to-Canonical Adaptation Networks), establishing the "sim-to-sim" paradigm. A generator network is trained entirely in simulation to convert domain-randomized images into their canonical (non-randomized) equivalents. At deployment, real images are fed through this same generator, producing clean simulated representations. The approach nearly doubled the grasping success of DR alone (70% vs. 36% zero-shot) and with just 5,000 real grasps of fine-tuning matched the performance of a system trained on 580,000 real grasps. The elegance of RCAN is that it requires no real data for the adaptation network itself, only for optional fine-tuning.

[28] identified a critical flaw in naive GAN-based adaptation: CycleGAN (an unpaired image-to-image translation framework that learns bidirectional mappings between two visual domains using cycle-consistency constraints) may alter or remove features that the downstream RL policy depends on, such as deleting graspable objects or shifting the robot arm's apparent position. RL-CycleGAN addresses this by adding an RL-consistency loss that penalizes changes in Q-values (the RL policy's estimate of expected future reward) across translated images. The Q-function automatically encodes which visual features matter for the task, making the adaptation task-aware without manual feature engineering. This achieved 70% grasping success from simulation-only training, versus 61% for vanilla CycleGAN.

[13] took a different approach with RetinaGAN, using a pretrained object detector (EfficientDet) as the consistency constraint rather than a task-specific RL loss. This decouples the GAN from any particular downstream task, enabling a single trained GAN to transfer across grasping, pushing, and door-opening tasks without retraining. RetinaGAN achieved 80% grasping success and notably generalized to a door-opening task (96.6% success) despite the detector being trained on completely different objects. The trade-off versus RL-CycleGAN is generality versus task-specificity: RetinaGAN transfers across tasks but cannot optimize for task-specific nuances that a general object detector misses.

More recently, [24] moved beyond GAN-based pixel translation to neural rendering. COV-NeRF (Composable Object Volume Neural Radiance Field) reconstructs individual objects from real images into NeRF representations (neural networks that encode a 3D scene as a continuous volumetric function mapping 3D coordinates to color and density), then composes them into novel scenes using a physics simulator for plausible poses. This produces photorealistic RGB images with automatically consistent depth maps and segmentation masks, all from a single volumetric rendering. On bin-picking grasping, COV-NeRF achieved 92.9% success versus 70% for pure sim-to-real, representing a +22.9 percentage point improvement. The shift from 2D image translation to 3D-aware neural rendering ensures geometric consistency across all output modalities, a property that GAN-based methods cannot guarantee.

4.4 Privileged Learning and Teacher-Student Distillation

This family of methods exploits the unique advantage of simulation: access to information that is unavailable in the real world.

[27] established the asymmetric actor-critic, where the critic trains on full simulator state while the actor receives only RGBD images. Combined with domain randomization, this achieved 100% success (5/5) on pick, push, and block-move tasks with a Fetch robot, while all baselines (symmetric RL, behavior cloning, DAgger) scored 0-1/5. The paper demonstrated that separating the training signal (which can use privileged information) from the deployment policy (which cannot) is a powerful principle.

[7] generalized this into the two-stage "learning by cheating" pipeline. In autonomous driving, a bird's-eye-view privileged agent achieved 100% success on the CARLA benchmark, then a camera-only student trained by imitating this teacher achieved 89% in dense traffic, an 18 percentage point improvement over prior state of the art. The critical insight is that the privileged agent acts as a "white box" oracle that can be queried at any state, enabling on-policy DAgger training without a human expert and providing massively more training signal per example than standard imitation learning.

[22] adapted this pipeline for quadrupedal locomotion with dramatic real-world results. The teacher, trained with TRPO (Trust Region Policy Optimization, a policy gradient method that constrains update step sizes for stability), had access to ground-truth terrain profiles and contact states. The student used a TCN processing 2 seconds of proprioceptive history. Beyond successful transfer to diverse natural terrains, the paper revealed an emergent behavior: a "foot-trapping reflex" where the policy detects collision from proprioceptive history and increases foot clearance on subsequent steps, with no hand-designed reflex logic. The adaptive terrain curriculum, implemented via a particle filter that maintains a distribution over terrains of appropriate difficulty, was also influential.

[12] identified a fundamental limitation of two-stage distillation: the student encounters states the teacher never saw, causing compounding distribution shift errors. Their Historical Information Bottleneck (HIB) method replaces two-stage distillation with single-stage joint training. Rather than imitating the teacher's actions (which they prove fails when teacher and student have different observation spaces), HIB learns a compressed latent representation of privileged information directly from proprioceptive history via an information bottleneck objective (a regularization principle that retains only task-relevant information while discarding irrelevant details). This achieved 100% success navigating 0.6m-high stairs on a Unitree A1, where the two-stage baseline (DreamWaQ) completely failed.

[38] addressed another inefficiency: in the standard two-stage pipeline, all teacher experience is discarded during student training. Their L2T (Learn to Teach) framework co-trains teacher and student simultaneously, sharing a replay buffer. The student contributes a scheduled fraction of rollouts, exposing the teacher to states the student actually visits and closing the distribution mismatch. This achieves 50% faster training than two-stage learning while matching final performance, and was validated with zero-shot transfer on a Digit humanoid across 12+ challenging terrains.

4.5 Meta-Learning and Online Adaptation

Meta-learning (learning to learn) trains policies or models that can rapidly adapt to new conditions from small amounts of data, rather than being robust to all conditions simultaneously.

[25] introduced meta-learned dynamics models for sim-to-real adaptation. Using MAML (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning, an algorithm that learns an initialization of neural network parameters from which a few gradient steps produce good performance on new tasks), they trained a dynamics model prior across diverse simulated conditions, then adapted it online from recent experience. The model was 10x more sample-efficient than model-free approaches and was the first meta-RL algorithm deployed on a real robot, with a legged millirobot adapting within seconds to missing legs, novel terrains, and payload changes.

[5] explicitly framed sim-to-real as a meta-RL problem, pairing MAML-style meta-training across randomized simulator dynamics with a VAE-based trajectory generator (a Variational Autoencoder that encodes trajectories into a low-dimensional latent space, constraining exploration to safe motions). The key finding was that a policy explicitly trained for fast adaptation produces more stable domain adaptation than a domain-randomized policy fine-tuned with the same data. The DR baseline actually deteriorated during adaptation in some conditions, while the meta-learned policy consistently improved.

[19] identified a limitation of MAML: when deployment situations are highly diverse (e.g., a broken leg versus rocky terrain), no single initialization allows fast adaptation to all of them. FAMLE (Fast Adaptation through Meta-Learning Embeddings) meta-trains multiple initial starting points, each capturing a different region of the dynamics space, then selects the best one for the current situation via likelihood. On a physical Minitaur quadruped, FAMLE walked 4.8m in 60 seconds of interaction versus 1.6m for MAML and 0.3m for non-adaptive baselines.

[21] with RMA offered perhaps the most practical adaptation architecture. Rather than gradient-based meta-learning (which requires carefully choosing learning rates and numbers of adaptation steps), RMA trains a feedforward adaptation module that directly predicts a latent environment encoding from proprioceptive history. This is conceptually simpler, computationally cheaper, and adapts in under one second. The on-policy training of the adaptation module (feeding its own imperfect predictions back into the base policy during training) prevents the distribution mismatch that plagues two-stage approaches. RMA achieved 73.5% success in simulation versus 62.4% for domain randomization and 56.5% for system identification, and handled up to 12 kg payload on a 12 kg robot.

[18] recently pushed meta-learned adaptation to agile wheeled platforms, coupling MAML-pretrained dynamics models with uncertainty-aware MPPI (Model Predictive Path Integral control, a sampling-based model predictive control method). The ensemble of adapted models provides epistemic uncertainty estimates injected into the control cost function, preventing the controller from exploiting regions where the model has overfit to limited data. This matched domain-specific baselines in-distribution while dramatically outperforming them out-of-distribution (e.g., on plastic tires where the baseline crashed).

4.6 Real-to-Sim-to-Real

Rather than training in a generic simulator and hoping for transfer, this paradigm reconstructs the real deployment environment in simulation and trains directly in that digital twin.

[36] introduced RialTo, a practical pipeline for manipulation. From ~15 minutes of 3D scanning and ~15 real demonstrations, RialTo constructs a digital twin, collects privileged state trajectories via "inverse distillation" (running a vision-based policy inside simulation to automatically extract privileged state labels), fine-tunes with PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization, a policy gradient algorithm that clips the objective to prevent destructively large updates), and distills back to a point-cloud student. The result is 91% success under pose randomization and 77% with visual distractors, representing a ~67% robustness improvement over behavior cloning with the same demonstrations. A striking finding was that environment-specialized digital twins dramatically outperform diverse procedural scene generation (90% vs. 10% success), suggesting that targeted reconstruction beats broad diversity.

[23] applied similar principles to humanoid dexterous manipulation, introducing an automated real-to-sim tuning module that calibrates physics parameters in under 4 minutes by minimizing tracking error against real robot motions. Combined with a divide-and-conquer distillation scheme (train single-object specialists, then distill into a generalist Diffusion Policy, a policy architecture that models the action distribution as a denoising diffusion process), they achieved 90% sim-to-real transfer on in-distribution objects and 60-80% on novel objects. This is the first demonstration of robust sim-to-real bimanual manipulation on a humanoid with multi-fingered hands.

4.7 Human-in-the-Loop Approaches

[17] proposed TRANSIC, where human operators observe and correct a simulation-trained policy during real-world execution, and a gated residual policy learns from these corrections. The residual architecture (learning only the delta from the base policy's actions) avoids catastrophic forgetting while patching real-world failure modes with only 58-489 human corrections per task. This achieved 81% average success across four contact-rich furniture assembly tasks, a domain where pure sim-to-real has historically struggled. The practical insight is that humans implicitly resolve all types of sim-to-real gaps simultaneously through correction, without needing to identify or engineer around any specific gap.

4.8 Cotraining: Mixing Sim and Real Data

[37] conducted the most systematic study to date of cotraining (training a single policy on a mixture of simulated and real demonstration data). Across 50+ real-world policies and 1,000+ real trials of planar pushing, they found that cotraining improves success from 10% to 70% in the low-data regime (10 real demos) and that real data is ~1.8x more valuable per-sample than sim data. The most surprising finding was that some visual gap between sim and real is actually beneficial for cotraining because it lets the policy distinguish which domain it is operating in and thus which physics to expect. Removing the visual gap while a physics gap exists actually hurts performance. This challenges the assumption that minimizing all gaps is always desirable.

5. Software, Tools, and Practical Resources

Simulators and Frameworks

NVIDIA Isaac Lab is currently the most feature-complete GPU-accelerated sim-to-real framework, with 30+ environments, 16+ robot models, RTX-based sensor simulation, and integrations with RSL-RL, SKRL, RL Games, and Stable Baselines. It evolved from the Orbit framework and ETH Zurich's legged_gym. The main trade-off is a heavy proprietary dependency stack: Isaac Sim requires NVIDIA GPUs and the Omniverse platform (~30+ GB install). With ~6,800 stars and active corporate backing, it is the safer choice for production-oriented projects. License: BSD-3 (framework), Apache 2.0 (mimic extension); Isaac Sim itself is proprietary.

MuJoCo Playground is the open-source alternative from Google DeepMind, built on MuJoCo's MJX (JAX) and Warp (NVIDIA) backends. It provides 10 robot platforms, comprehensive domain randomization pipelines, and asymmetric actor-critic training, all trainable in minutes on a single GPU. It won the Outstanding Demo Paper Award at RSS 2025. With ~1,800 stars and active maintenance, it is younger but growing rapidly. The main limitation is less mature sim-to-real documentation compared to Isaac Lab, and no published quantitative transfer results from the framework itself. License: Apache-2.0.

legged_gym from ETH Zurich's Robotic Systems Lab was the reference codebase for quadruped sim-to-real RL research, accompanying [30]. Built on Isaac Gym Preview 3, it provides actuator network modeling, friction/mass randomization, noisy observations, and random pushes. With ~2,800 stars it remains widely used, but it is now officially deprecated in favor of Isaac Lab, and its dependency on older Python (3.6-3.8) and PyTorch (1.10) versions makes it increasingly difficult to maintain.

Humanoid-Gym extends the legged_gym paradigm to humanoid robots, featuring a Denoising World Model Learning (DWL) approach that was an RSS 2024 Best Paper Award Finalist. Its unique three-tier validation pipeline (Isaac Gym training, MuJoCo sim2sim verification, zero-shot hardware deployment) provides an extra sanity check before real-world transfer. With ~1,900 stars it has moderate community adoption, though the 37-commit count suggests a research release rather than an actively maintained product.

Walk These Ways from MIT provides an end-to-end pipeline for the Unitree Go1, implementing the Multiplicity of Behavior (MoB) framework that trains a single policy conditioned on a continuous command space of gait parameters. With ~1,300 stars, it is a good starting point for researchers with Go1 hardware, though its sim-to-real techniques are relatively basic (standard DR without system identification or dynamics adaptation).

CARLA remains the de facto standard for autonomous driving sim-to-real research, with over 6,100 citations. Built on Unreal Engine 4, it provides configurable sensors, weather, and traffic for urban driving scenarios. For closing the visual gap, CARLA2Real applies the photorealism enhancement technique of [29] to translate CARLA renders toward the style of real-world driving datasets at ~13 FPS.

Benchmarks and Evaluation

SimplerEnv is a simulation-based evaluation framework for generalist manipulation policies, built on SAPIEN. It provides Visual Matching and Variant Aggregation evaluation setups that correlate with real-world performance across 9 manipulation tasks. Its Mean Maximum Rank Violation (MMRV) metric measures policy ranking consistency between sim and real, addressing limitations of simple correlation measures. With ~1,000 stars and support for RT-1, RT-1-X, and Octo policies, it is the best current option for evaluating manipulation policies without physical hardware.

Surveys and Reading Lists

AwesomeSim2Real is the most comprehensive curated list of sim-to-real RL literature, organized by MDP component (observation, action, transition, reward). It accompanies the [9] survey and covers ~100+ papers and resources. The MDP-component taxonomy is a useful organizational framework.

Lilian Weng's blog post "Domain Randomization for Sim2Real Transfer" (2019) remains one of the most accessible introductions to domain randomization, covering uniform DR, guided/automatic DR, and network-driven randomization with clear diagrams and mathematical intuitions.

The CMU 10-703 Deep Reinforcement Learning and Control course (2024) includes dedicated lecture slides on sim-to-real transfer covering domain randomization, system identification, and maximum-entropy RL for robust policies.

Getting Started

For a researcher entering the field today, the recommended setup depends on the target domain:

  • Legged locomotion: Start with MuJoCo Playground or Isaac Lab. Both provide ready-to-train quadruped environments with DR pipelines. MuJoCo Playground is simpler to install; Isaac Lab offers more robot models and sensor fidelity.
  • Dexterous manipulation: Isaac Lab is the stronger choice due to its RTX-rendered cameras and broader manipulation environment catalog.
  • Autonomous driving: CARLA remains the standard. Install CARLA2Real for visual gap reduction.
  • Benchmarking manipulation policies: SimplerEnv with the ManiSkill3 GPU backend.

6. State of the Art

What works best today

For legged locomotion, the combination of privileged teacher-student distillation with either domain randomization or precise system identification achieves reliable zero-shot transfer. The evidence from [6] suggests that when accurate actuator models are feasible (requiring ~20 seconds of encoder data per joint), system identification outperforms domain randomization in terms of both transfer quality and energy efficiency. However, domain randomization remains the pragmatic default when actuator modeling effort is not justified or when the robot platform lacks torque-sensing capabilities.

For dexterous manipulation, ADR-style progressive randomization combined with memory-augmented policies (LSTMs or Transformers) represents the current frontier, as demonstrated by [1] and [23]. The divide-and-conquer distillation approach of [23], training single-object specialists then distilling into a generalist Diffusion Policy, appears to significantly outperform monolithic multi-object training (90% vs. 23.3% sim-to-real success).

For vision-based manipulation with limited real data, the real-to-sim-to-real paradigm [36] and cotraining [37] both show strong results. RialTo's environment-specialized digital twins provide 2.5x higher success rates than behavior cloning while using fewer demonstrations. Cotraining can provide 2-7x improvement with as few as 10 real demonstrations.

For autonomous driving, privileged teacher-student distillation [7] combined with visual domain adaptation [29] is the dominant paradigm, though the CARLA-to-real-vehicle transfer gap remains larger than in robotics due to the safety-critical nature and the difficulty of testing at scale.

Points of consensus

The field broadly agrees on several principles:

  1. Memory matters. Recurrent or history-conditioned policies transfer better than reactive ones, both empirically [26, 3] and theoretically [8]. The implicit system identification performed by LSTM hidden states is a key mechanism for adaptation.
  2. Privileged information during training is a feature, not a limitation. The asymmetric actor-critic and teacher-student distillation paradigms have become standard practice across locomotion, manipulation, and driving.
  3. Diversity beats realism for visual transfer. Deliberately unrealistic, randomly-textured scenes transfer better than painstakingly crafted photorealistic ones [35, 31]. This is the single most counterintuitive finding in the field.
  4. The dynamics gap is harder than the visual gap. Multiple studies confirm that physics-level mismatches are more damaging than visual mismatches for contact-rich tasks [37, 2].

Points of disagreement

  1. Domain randomization versus system identification. [6] argue that precise actuator identification makes DR unnecessary for locomotion, while the DR community argues that randomization provides robustness to unmodeled effects that identification misses. The answer likely depends on the quality of the parametric model: if the model structure is correct and parameters are identifiable, ID wins; if important effects are structurally absent from the model, DR provides a safety net.
  2. Single-stage versus two-stage privileged learning. [12] and [38] both argue for single-stage training to avoid distribution shift, but two-stage approaches remain dominant in practice due to their simplicity and the ease of reusing existing teacher policies.
  3. How much real data is needed. Zero-shot methods aim for none; cotraining and meta-learning methods argue for a small amount (10-100 trajectories); some practitioners argue that the field overvalues zero-shot and that a small real-world calibration set is always worth the effort.

7. Open Problems and Future Directions

Deformable and fluid dynamics

Virtually all successful sim-to-real transfer has been on rigid or quasi-rigid bodies. Deformable objects (cloth, rope, dough), granular materials (sand, rice), and fluids remain fundamentally challenging because their dynamics are computationally expensive to simulate and highly sensitive to initial conditions. [2] identify this as perhaps the single largest remaining barrier to general-purpose sim-to-real transfer.

Contact-rich manipulation at precision

Despite the OpenAI Rubik's Cube result, fine-grained contact-rich manipulation (assembly, tool use, insertion with tight tolerances) remains difficult. [17] achieve only 45% on their hardest insertion task, and [23] report only 52.5% on bimanual handover. The challenge is that small parameter errors in contact dynamics (friction, stiffness, damping) produce qualitatively different outcomes during tight-tolerance tasks, and current DR ranges cannot cover this sensitivity without making the task intractably difficult to learn.

Unified observation representations for video-to-state-to-control

A core question highly relevant to the "state is the interface" thesis: what is the right state representation for bridging video-based perception and physics-based control? The field currently uses 6DoF object poses, keypoints, point clouds, and latent embeddings, with no consensus on which is best. The sim-to-real gap compounds this question because the chosen representation must be estimable from real sensors with sufficient accuracy to drive contact-aware control.

Video generation models that predict future states could serve as planners, but they must predict states that are both physically plausible (satisfying contact constraints) and estimable from real observations. 3D Gaussian Splatting as a state representation is an intriguing direction: it is geometrically rich, differentiable, and can be reconstructed from real images, but its utility for contact prediction and control remains undemonstrated.

Differentiable simulation

End-to-end gradient-based optimization through physics simulation (using frameworks like NVIDIA Warp, Taichi, or Brax) could in principle close the sim-to-real gap by directly optimizing simulator parameters to match real-world outcomes. In practice, differentiable simulators struggle with contact discontinuities (where the gradient is undefined or uninformative), and the computational cost of backpropagating through long simulation horizons remains prohibitive. This is an active research frontier with significant potential.

Foundation model integration

The use of VLMs and LLMs for sim-to-real is in its infancy. [40] showed that natural language can serve as a domain-invariant bridge for visual transfer, and [9] catalog early work on LLM-generated reward functions and LLM-enhanced dynamics models. The promise is that pretrained foundation models encode vast world knowledge that could help specify what aspects of simulation matter and what the real world actually looks like. The risk is hallucination: an LLM generating a physically incorrect reward function could train a dangerous policy. Grounding foundation model outputs in physical constraints remains an open challenge.

Scaling to long-horizon tasks

Most sim-to-real successes involve short-horizon skills (grasping, pushing, walking a few steps). Chaining these into long-horizon tasks (cooking a meal, assembling furniture, navigating a building) requires handling error accumulation over many steps, where small per-step sim-to-real discrepancies compound. [17] demonstrate skill chaining for furniture assembly, but success rates degrade multiplicatively. Video generation models as sequence-of-state-goal planners could address this by providing long-horizon guidance, but the sim-to-real gap for video models themselves is an additional complication.

Standardized evaluation and metrics

The field lacks standardized metrics for quantifying the sim-to-real gap itself, as opposed to downstream task performance. [2] propose several candidates: Sim-to-Real Correlation Coefficient (SRCC), offline replay error, visual fidelity scores (FID, SSIM), and transfer performance metrics. [4] introduce the Maximum Normalized Cross-Correlation (MNCC) for measuring reality alignment alongside a two-level metric suite. Adoption of such metrics would enable systematic comparison across methods and platforms, which is currently impossible.

8. Recommended Reading Order

For a researcher entering the sim-to-real field, the following five papers provide a coherent progression from foundational concepts to the current state of the art:

  1. [35] "Domain Randomization for Transferring Deep Neural Networks from Simulation to the Real World." Start here. This is the most-cited paper in the field, clearly written, and establishes the core principle that diversity beats realism. The ablation studies are exemplary.
  2. [26] "Sim-to-Real Transfer of Robotic Control with Dynamics Randomization." Extends randomization from vision to physics and introduces the critical idea that LSTM memory enables implicit system identification, the mechanism underlying most successful sim-to-real methods.
  3. [22] "Learning Quadrupedal Locomotion over Challenging Terrain." The canonical example of the privileged teacher-student pipeline, with dramatic real-world results. Reading this paper gives you the full modern sim-to-real recipe: terrain curriculum, privileged teacher, temporal student, domain randomization, and zero-shot deployment.
  4. [21] "RMA: Rapid Motor Adaptation for Legged Robots." Introduces the adaptation module paradigm that separates base policy from environmental inference, enabling real-time adaptation. The two-phase training scheme is both simple and effective.
  5. [8] "Understanding Domain Randomization for Sim-to-real Transfer." Provides the theoretical foundations. After reading the empirical papers above, this one explains why they work, what conditions are necessary, and where the limits lie.

Tools to install first:

  • MuJoCo (free, open-source, pip-installable) for quick experimentation
  • Isaac Lab when ready for GPU-accelerated parallel training and sensor-rich environments
  • SimplerEnv for benchmarking manipulation policies

For autonomous driving researchers, start with CARLA and read [7] in place of papers 3-4 above.
